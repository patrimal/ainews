Karpathy hasn’t written code since December, describes agent era as “state of psychosis”

Yesterday Fortune / No Priors podcast

In a No Priors podcast interview covered by Fortune, Andrej Karpathy revealed he stopped writing code entirely in December as AI agents took over his workflow. “I don’t think a normal person actually realizes that this happened or how dramatic it was,” he said. He described being “in the state of psychosis of trying to figure out what’s possible, trying to push it to the limit” — and wanting to be at the forefront but feeling “extremely nervous” that he’s not. He also revealed “Dobby the House Elf” — an OpenClaw-based agent controlling his home’s lighting, HVAC, security, pool, and spa via WhatsApp.

Fortune’s framing: this isn’t a hobbyist playing with toys — it’s a former OpenAI co-founder saying the shift from writing code to directing agents was the most dramatic change in his career, and three months in, he’s still trying to process what it means.

OpenClaw’s “ChatGPT moment” is making AI models a commodity

Yesterday CNBC

CNBC reports that OpenClaw has become so popular it’s exposing how rapidly expensive proprietary AI models are becoming commoditized. Developers are running fleets of always-on AI agents on personal Mac Minis, finding it far more economical than cloud APIs. Nvidia responded by building free NemoClaw security services to get enterprises comfortable with adoption. Both OpenAI and Anthropic are scrambling — Anthropic recently shipped OpenClaw-like features including its new --channels permission relay.

CNBC’s argument: if the most capable AI agent framework is open-source and runs on a $600 desktop, the value is migrating from the model layer to the agent layer — exactly the shift that threatens the business models companies just raised hundreds of billions to build.

OpenAI plans to nearly double headcount to 8,000 by year-end

Yesterday Financial Times / Bloomberg

OpenAI plans to grow from 4,500 to 8,000 employees by end-2026, per the Financial Times. New hires span product, engineering, research, and sales — plus a new category of “technical ambassadors” to help enterprise customers use its AI tools. The hiring push comes as OpenAI approaches an $840 billion valuation following its $110 billion raise.

Fortune’s framing: this is explicitly competitive defense against Anthropic and Google, not organic growth. OpenAI is spending its war chest to lock in enterprise customers before the commoditization wave — exemplified by OpenClaw — erodes its moat.
