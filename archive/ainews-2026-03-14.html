The Big Picture

Slow news Sunday? Hardly. Altman went to a room full of BlackRock investors, warned them AI is “disrupting the basic fabric of capitalism,” and then — without a trace of irony — pitched them on buying intelligence from OpenAI on a meter. The man is speedrunning the “we come in peace / that’ll be $49.99” pipeline. Karpathy, meanwhile, did something actually useful: he scored every US occupation on AI exposure and the numbers are stark. 42% of workers in the danger zone. Not vibes, not think-piece hand-wringing — real BLS data mapped to a 0–10 scale you can argue with. That’s the kind of work that moves a conversation forward. The genuinely exciting thing this weekend is tomorrow: GTC kicks off and NVIDIA is expected to unveil Vera Rubin. If the 5x inference claims hold up, that’s not incremental — that’s a step function. LeCun raising a billion for JEPA is bold and fascinating, though the “LLMs are a dead end” thesis is starting to feel like it needs results, not just funding rounds. And the layoff numbers — 45,000 in March alone — are the kind of thing that makes the rest of this news feel less abstract and more urgent.

Altman at BlackRock: AI is “disrupting the basic fabric of capitalism”

Today Sam Altman (@sama)

Altman’s remarks at the BlackRock U.S. Infrastructure Summit went viral today. He warned that “the next few years are going to be a painful adjustment” as the labor-capital balance shifts, said companies are already blaming AI for layoffs “whether or not it really is about AI,” and predicted that government will become “the insurer of last resort.” He also pitched intelligence as a metered utility — “too cheap to meter” — with OpenAI as the provider.

Altman’s framing: he’s simultaneously warning about economic disruption and positioning OpenAI to be the utility company that profits from it. The backlash was immediate — critics noted the tension between “painful adjustment” and “buy it from us on a meter.”

Karpathy’s AI job exposure map: 42% of US workers in the danger zone

Today Andrej Karpathy (@karpathy)

Karpathy published an interactive treemap at karpathy.ai/jobs scoring all 342 US occupations on AI exposure (0–10 scale) using BLS employment data. Average exposure: 5.3. Software developers land at 8–9, medical transcriptionists hit a perfect 10, trades like plumbing sit at 0–1. Musk responded on X: “all jobs will be optional.”

Karpathy’s core finding: if your entire job lives on a screen, AI is coming for it. 59.9 million workers — 42% of US employment — score 7+. He highlights prompt skill as the key differentiator that can materially lower individual risk within the same job title.

NVIDIA GTC 2026 kicks off tomorrow — Vera Rubin and NemoClaw expected

Today The Rundown AI / CNBC

Jensen Huang’s keynote on March 16 is expected to formally unveil the Vera Rubin platform (5x inference, 3.5x training over Blackwell), NemoClaw (an open-source enterprise AI agent platform already being pitched to Salesforce and Google), and new N1/N1X ARM-based CPUs for Windows laptops. The CPU is sharing top billing with the GPU this year.

NVIDIA is betting that the next bottleneck isn’t just GPU compute but the full stack — CPU, networking, and an open agent platform to run on top of it. NemoClaw is their play to own the enterprise agent layer, not just the silicon.

Claude Code v2.1.76: MCP elicitation, /effort command, sparse worktrees

Yesterday Claude Code Changelog

Friday’s release adds MCP elicitation support (servers can request structured input mid-task via interactive dialogs), the /effort slash command for setting model effort level, -n flag for named sessions, and worktree.sparsePaths for large monorepos. Also adds PostCompact hooks and fixes for deferred tools losing schemas after compaction.

MCP elicitation closes a real gap: servers can now ask for human input during execution instead of failing silently or guessing. It’s the kind of plumbing that makes agent-driven workflows actually viable in production.

LeCun’s AMI Labs closes $1.03B seed — Europe’s largest ever

Mar 10 Yann LeCun / TechCrunch

Yann LeCun’s startup Advanced Machine Intelligence (AMI) Labs raised $1.03 billion at a $3.5B pre-money valuation, backed by Bezos Expeditions, Nvidia, Toyota, Samsung, and Eric Schmidt among others. AMI is building world models based on LeCun’s Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture (JEPA), targeting robotics, industrial, and healthcare applications.

LeCun is putting a billion-dollar bet behind his long-standing argument that autoregressive LLMs are a dead end for real-world reasoning. If JEPA works, it reframes the entire industry; if not, it’s the most expensive “I told you so” attempt in AI history.

AI layoffs accelerate: Oracle plans 20K–30K cuts, Block already shed 40%

Mar 9 The Neuron / Bloomberg

Oracle is planning 20,000–30,000 layoffs (12–18% of workforce) to redirect $8–10B toward AI data center infrastructure. Block already cut ~4,000 employees (~40%), with Jack Dorsey explicitly citing AI and predicting most companies would follow within a year. Total 2026 tech layoffs hit 45,000 in March alone, with 9,200+ directly attributed to AI.

Dorsey’s bluntness is the signal: “most companies will make similar cuts within a year.” Oracle’s version is even starker — they’re not just cutting to save money, they’re redirecting those billions into the infrastructure that replaces the people they’re firing.
