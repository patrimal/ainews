Karpathy maps AI exposure for every US job — 42% score high risk

Today Andrej Karpathy (@karpathy)

Karpathy published an interactive AI job exposure map at karpathy.ai/jobs, scoring 342 US occupations on a 0–10 scale using BLS data and LLM assessment. The average exposure is 5.3 out of 10. Software developers land at 8–9, medical transcriptionists hit a perfect 10, while trades like plumbing and roofing sit at 0–1. Visualized as a treemap where size is employment volume and color is exposure level.

The core pattern: 42% of all US jobs (59.9 million workers) score 7 or higher — essentially any role that lives entirely on a screen. Karpathy highlights prompt skill as the key differentiator that can materially lower individual risk within the same job title.

Willison: “Tests are no longer remotely optional” in the age of coding agents

Yesterday Simon Willison (@simonw)

Willison gave a fireside chat on agentic engineering at the Pragmatic Summit in San Francisco. He outlined the progression from asking ChatGPT questions to agents writing more code than humans, and argued that test-driven development is now mandatory — not optional — when working with agents. He also called Opus 4.5 “the first model that earned my trust” for familiar problem classes.

Willison’s framing: the shift from code review to test suites as the primary trust mechanism. When agents write most of the code, reading every line stops scaling — red-green TDD becomes your verification layer.

GitHub’s “slopocalypse” forces Jazzband to shut down open membership

Yesterday Simon Willison (@simonw)

Jazzband, a collaborative Python open-source project built on shared push access, announced it can no longer sustain its open membership model. Maintainer Jannis Leidel says the flood of AI-generated spam PRs and issues has made the model untenable — only 1 in 10 AI-authored PRs meet basic quality standards. Willison shared the quote alongside references to curl’s closed bug bounty and GitHub’s new PR kill switch.

Leidel’s point: permissive access models designed for human-scale collaboration can’t survive when the majority of automated contributions are low-quality noise — a structural problem, not a moderation one.

Morgan Stanley: US faces 9–18 GW power shortfall from AI infrastructure through 2028

Mar 13 Morgan Stanley via Fortune

Morgan Stanley’s “Intelligence Factory” model projects a 12%–25% deficit in the power needed to run US AI infrastructure through 2028. Developers are already converting Bitcoin mining operations into AI compute centers and deploying natural gas turbines and fuel cells rather than waiting for grid upgrades. The bank calls AI a “powerful deflationary force” as tools replicate human work at a fraction of cost.

The bank’s thesis: the AI compute buildout is outrunning the electrical grid — whoever solves power access first gets to set the pace of the next generation of model training.
