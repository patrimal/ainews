DOJ appeals Anthropic ban ruling — federal court will decide if government can blacklist AI companies over safety demands

Today Bloomberg / Seeking Alpha

The Trump administration filed a notice of appeal today challenging Judge Rita Lin’s March 26 order that blocked the government’s ban on Anthropic AI across all federal agencies. The ban stemmed from Anthropic’s refusal to drop safety guardrails — specifically a restriction prohibiting mass surveillance of American citizens — during Pentagon contract negotiations. Judge Lin had called the ban “classic illegal First Amendment retaliation” and found the national security justification questionable. The DOJ has one week before the stay expires.

Bloomberg’s framing: this is the first federal appeals case testing whether the government can designate an AI company a “supply chain risk” as retaliation for publicly insisting on ethical red lines. The outcome sets precedent for every AI company negotiating with the Pentagon.

LeCun at Brown: “If you are interested in human-level AI, don’t work on LLMs”

Yesterday Brown Daily Herald / Brown University

Yann LeCun delivered his most provocative public lecture yet at Brown’s Lemley Family Leadership Series. He displayed a red all-caps slide reading “IF YOU ARE INTERESTED IN HUMAN-LEVEL AI, DON’T WORK ON LLMs” and told a capacity crowd that “hundreds of billions invested in an industry counting on LLMs reaching human-level intelligence” is “complete BS.” He argued LLMs manipulate language to seem smart but are “completely helpless when it comes to the physical world” and “intrinsically unsafe.” His alternative: world models trained on sensory data that can predict consequences of actions — the bet behind his $3.5B startup AMI Labs.

LeCun’s core argument: intelligence means accomplishing new tasks with no prior training, which LLMs cannot do. The unsolved problem he identified — hierarchical planning — is what his billion-dollar company is built to crack. It’s a genuine technical thesis, not just contrarianism, but he now has $3.5B riding on being right.

Anthropic’s Claude Code source leaks via npm — 512K lines expose anti-distillation tricks, “undercover mode,” and unreleased KAIROS autonomous agent

Yesterday BleepingComputer / TechCrunch / Gizmodo / @bcherny

A misconfigured npm publish of Claude Code v2.1.88 on March 31 shipped a 60MB cli.js.map source map containing ~1,900 files and ~512,000 lines of reconstructable TypeScript. The repo was forked 41,500+ times within hours. Developers found anti-distillation poison pills (fake tool definitions injected to corrupt competitor training data), a frustration regex detecting user profanity, native client attestation acting as DRM for API calls, and “undercover mode” that strips all Anthropic traces from employees’ open-source contributions. Most intriguing: KAIROS, an unreleased persistent agent with background daemons, GitHub webhooks, a /dream mode for memory distillation, and 5-minute cron loops. Anthropic’s DMCA response accidentally took down 8,100+ innocent GitHub repos before Boris Cherny retracted the bulk notices, limiting takedowns to 96 forks of the actual leaked code.

The strategic damage isn’t the code — it’s the roadmap. Competitors can now anticipate features like persistent autonomous agents months before Anthropic ships them, and the anti-distillation bypass (CLAUDE_CODE_DISABLE_EXPERIMENTAL_BETAS) is now public knowledge. Gizmodo notes the timing is brutal: this hits as Anthropic reportedly prepares for a potential IPO.
