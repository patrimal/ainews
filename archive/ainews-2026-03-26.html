Today Anthropic / Claude Code changelog

Arm ships its first chip in 35 years: a 136-core AI data center CPU

Yesterday Arm Newsroom / Tom’s Hardware / CNBC

Arm launched the AGI CPU, its first in-house production processor after 35 years of licensing IP to partners. Built on TSMC’s 3nm process with up to 136 Neoverse V3 cores at 3.2 GHz all-core (3.7 GHz boost) within a 300W TDP, the chip was co-developed with Meta as lead partner. Arm claims 2x performance per rack versus x86, enabling up to $10B in CAPEX savings per GW of data center capacity. Launch partners include Meta, OpenAI, Cerebras, and Cloudflare. Arm stock jumped 16% on the news.

Tom’s Hardware: this is the first time Arm has competed directly with its own licensees, a fundamental shift in the company’s business model. The $15B annual revenue projection from this chip alone signals Arm believes the inference compute market is large enough to justify alienating partners — and Meta’s co-development role suggests at least one hyperscaler agrees.

AI models solve open math conjecture for the first time on FrontierMath

Mar 24 Epoch AI / WinBuzzer

GPT-5.4 Pro produced a novel solution to a Ramsey-style problem on hypergraphs that had been open since a 2019 paper by Will Brian and Paul Larson. The construction, establishing H(n) ≥ (26/25)·k_n for n ≥ 15, was elicited by researchers Kevin Barreto and Liam Price using a prompting workflow they’d developed for Erdős-type problems. Problem contributor Will Brian confirmed the result as publishable. Epoch AI subsequently verified that Opus 4.6, Gemini 3.1 Pro, and GPT-5.4 (xhigh) could also solve it, marking the first time any model has cleared FrontierMath’s open-problem track.

Epoch AI’s framing: FrontierMath scores jumped from ~5% under GPT-4 in 2024 to 50% under GPT-5.4 Pro — but solving an open problem is categorically different from solving known ones. This is the first independently verified instance of AI producing novel mathematics that extends human knowledge, not just reproducing it.

OpenAI in talks to buy 50 GW of fusion power from Helion Energy

Mar 23 TechCrunch / Axios / GeekWire

OpenAI is negotiating a deal with Helion Energy for 5 GW of power by 2030, ramping to 50 GW by 2035 — roughly 12.5% of Helion’s projected total output. Sam Altman, who led Helion’s $500M round in 2021 with a personal $375M investment, stepped down as Helion’s board chair to clear the conflict of interest. Helion’s first commercial fusion plant is planned for 2028, though the company has yet to demonstrate commercial viability.

TechCrunch’s read: the deal is as much a signal about AI’s energy problem as it is about fusion — OpenAI plans to spend $100B+ on infrastructure and needs power sources that don’t exist yet. Altman stepping down from the Helion board is notable corporate governance, but the underlying bet is on a reactor that hasn’t generated net energy commercially.
