Altman attacker charged with attempted murder — carried a hit list of AI executives’ home addresses

Today NPR / CNN / The Register / Al Jazeera

San Francisco DA Brooke Jenkins charged Daniel Moreno-Gama, 20, of Spring, Texas with two counts of attempted murder and nine additional charges for the April 10 Molotov cocktail attack on Sam Altman’s Russian Hill home. Court filings reveal Moreno-Gama carried a document listing names and home addresses of other AI company CEOs, board members, and investors. After firebombing Altman’s gate at 3:45 AM, he traveled to OpenAI’s Mission Bay headquarters and tried to batter his way in with a chair while carrying a kerosene jug and additional incendiary devices. Federal charges for explosives and unregistered firearms are running in parallel.

NPR’s reporting: the hit list with home addresses of AI executives transforms this from an isolated act into a documented targeting campaign against industry leadership. State charges carry 19 years to life; federal mandatory minimums add 5–20 more. The FBI has already raided a connected residence in Texas.

Claude Managed Agents public beta triggers “SaaS-pocalypse” — $2T wiped from software stocks

Apr 11 24/7 Wall St / Seeking Alpha / The New Stack

Anthropic launched Claude Managed Agents in public beta on April 8 — a fully managed harness where Claude runs as an autonomous agent with secure sandboxing, tool execution, web browsing, and code execution for $0.08/session-hour plus standard token rates. Notion, Rakuten, and Sentry are already in production. The market response was brutal: Fastly fell 18%, Akamai dropped 16.6%, Cloudflare lost 13.5%, DigitalOcean slid 13.4%. The selloff, which began with Claude Cowork in January, has now erased approximately $2 trillion from software market capitalization.

24/7 Wall St’s analysis: Managed Agents bundles code execution, credential management, and hosting into a single API call, compressing the seat-based SaaS model by replacing work that once required multiple licensed tools and human operators. The companies hit hardest — CDN, edge, and hosting providers — sell exactly the infrastructure Anthropic is now packaging for free.

Meta Muse Spark: first model from Superintelligence Labs is closed-source

Apr 8 Bloomberg / CNBC / TechCrunch / Gizmodo

Meta Superintelligence Labs, led by Chief AI Officer Alexandr Wang, shipped Muse Spark on April 8 — a natively multimodal reasoning model with tool use, visual chain of thought, and a “Contemplating mode” that orchestrates multiple agents reasoning in parallel. The team rebuilt Meta’s AI stack from the ground up in nine months. The headline: Muse Spark is closed-source, breaking with Meta’s years-long open-model strategy that produced Llama. Available now at meta.ai.

CNBC’s framing: this is Meta’s first major model since the $14B deal that brought in Alexandr Wang, and the closed-source decision marks the end of Meta as the corporate champion of open AI. Gizmodo was less kind — the model “doesn’t exactly spark joy” — but the strategic shift matters more than the benchmarks.

Anthropic cuts OpenClaw from Claude subscriptions — Steinberger heads to OpenAI

Apr 4 TechCrunch / Boris Cherny / VentureBeat

Starting April 4 at noon PT, Claude Pro and Max subscriptions no longer cover usage through third-party tools including OpenClaw. Users must now pay separately via “extra usage” billing. Boris Cherny explained that “subscriptions weren’t built for the usage patterns of these third-party tools” and that Anthropic is “prioritizing our customers using our products and API.” He also submitted PRs to improve prompt cache efficiency for OpenClaw specifically. OpenClaw creator Peter Steinberger and board member Dave Morin said they “tried to talk sense into Anthropic” but could only delay the pricing by one week. Steinberger subsequently announced he’s joining OpenAI. Anthropic offered a one-time credit equal to monthly subscription price, redeemable through April 17.

Cherny’s own tweets: the capacity framing is honest — OpenClaw sessions consumed far more compute than native Claude Code sessions. But VentureBeat’s reporting adds the competitive layer: Steinberger’s immediate departure to OpenAI and the one-week delay after lobbying reveal this wasn’t purely an engineering decision. Karpathy’s observation that the OpenClaw moment was big because “non-technical people experienced agentic models for the first time” gives context for the demand spike.
