Anthropic cuts off third-party tools from Claude subscriptions — OpenClaw and others lose access

Today Updated 2:00 PM The Decoder / VentureBeat / XDA

The cutoff is now live. As of noon PT, Claude Pro and Max subscriptions no longer work through third-party harnesses like OpenClaw. Users must opt into pay-as-you-go “extra usage” billing or use the API directly. Anthropic is offering a one-time credit equal to one month’s subscription and up to 30% discounts on pre-purchased usage bundles. OpenClaw creator Peter Steinberger negotiated a brief delay but called the move “sad for the ecosystem,” alleging Anthropic “absorbed popular features from his software into its closed system, then shut out alternatives.”

Steinberger’s point: flat-rate subscriptions fundamentally clash with agent-driven continuous usage patterns. This is the first major AI platform drawing that line — and the timing with Claude Code’s feature absorption isn’t coincidental.

OpenAI fully retires GPT-4o from all plans — 0.1% of users remained

Yesterday OpenAI / CNBC

OpenAI completed the full retirement of GPT-4o from ChatGPT on April 3, ending access for the final holdouts: Business, Enterprise, and Edu customers using it within Custom GPTs. The API remains available for now. Internal metrics showed only 0.1% of daily ChatGPT users still selected GPT-4o when the February deprecation was announced — usage had migrated overwhelmingly to GPT-5.2, 5.3, and the 5.4 family.

OpenAI’s own data: GPT-4o was the model that brought multimodal to the mainstream — its quiet death with 99.9% of users already gone marks how fast the frontier moves. Under two years from flagship to footnote.

Newsom signs executive order asserting California’s AI oversight — overrules federal Anthropic ban

Apr 2 CalMatters / Washington Post

Governor Newsom signed an executive order establishing that California will independently review federal supply-chain risk designations before deciding whether to do business with flagged AI companies. This directly addresses the DOD’s designation of Anthropic as a supply-chain risk, which blocked the company from military contracts over clauses banning domestic mass surveillance and autonomous weapons. The order also requires state agencies to assess AI harm in contracting decisions while encouraging AI adoption.

Washington Post’s framing: this reframes the Anthropic ban as a states’-rights issue. California is asserting tech sovereignty — deciding which AI companies are safe to work with rather than deferring to a federal designation the state considers politically motivated.

Google launches Gemma 4 — four open-weight models under Apache 2.0, built from Gemini 3

Apr 2 Google / The Register

Google released Gemma 4: four open-weight models from edge to workstation. The lineup spans 2B and 4B “Effective” models for smartphones, a 26B Mixture-of-Experts model, and a 31B Dense model. Built from Gemini 3 research, they feature 256K context windows, native vision and audio, function calling, and 140+ language support. The big shift: Apache 2.0 licensing replaces Gemma 3’s restrictive terms.

The Register’s analysis: this is Google’s direct counter to the Chinese open-weight models (DeepSeek, Qwen) eating its lunch. Apache 2.0 removes the enterprise licensing friction that held back Gemma 3 adoption.
