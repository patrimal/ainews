Meta planning 20% layoffs (~15,800 jobs) to fund AI infrastructure binge

Today CNBC / Reuters

Meta stock climbed 3.2% premarket to $633 on Monday after Reuters reported the company is planning to cut 20%+ of its ~79,000 workforce. The cuts would offset $135 billion in planned 2026 capital expenditure — roughly double last year — mostly on AI data centers. Meta spokesperson Andy Stone called it “speculative reporting about theoretical approaches.” If confirmed, it would be Meta’s largest reduction since the 2022–2023 “year of efficiency.”

CNBC’s framing says it all: stock goes up on mass layoffs. Meta is doubling its capex to $135B while cutting 20% of its humans. The market is pricing in a future where fewer people and more GPUs is the winning formula.

Karpathy’s AI job exposure map goes viral, then he walks it back

Yesterday Andrej Karpathy (@karpathy)

Karpathy published an interactive treemap Saturday scoring all 342 US occupations on AI exposure (0–10) using BLS data. Key numbers: average exposure 5.3, jobs paying $100K+ average 6.7, jobs under $35K average 3.4. It went viral — then Sunday morning he deleted the GitHub repo and clarified: “This was a Saturday morning 2-hour vibe coded project inspired by a book I’m reading.” The site at karpathy.ai/jobs is still live.

Fortune’s headline nails it: “the highest-paying jobs have the worst scores.” Karpathy walking it back as a casual weekend hack doesn’t change the underlying BLS data — white-collar workers earning six figures are the most exposed, and the numbers are still public.

Tesla Terafab: Musk’s $25B AI chip factory launches this week

Yesterday Tom’s Hardware / Teslarati

Musk announced Saturday that Tesla’s Terafab AI chip manufacturing project launches in seven days (~March 21–22). The facility will vertically integrate logic processing, memory, and advanced packaging under one roof — a scale no private company outside Taiwan and South Korea currently operates. First product: Tesla’s fifth-gen AI5 chip for autonomous driving, with small-batch production in 2026 and volume in 2027. Estimated cost: $25 billion, part of Tesla’s $20B+ capex plan for 2026.

Tom’s Hardware calls it Musk’s “latest moonshot” — but the strategic logic is clear: Tesla wants to stop depending on external fabs for the chips that power FSD. If Terafab works, Tesla controls its own silicon destiny. That’s a genuine competitive moat.
