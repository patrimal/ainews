The one-person billion-dollar company is here — and the FDA got there first

Yesterday NYT / PYMNTS / NewsNation / Sam Altman on X

Matthew Gallagher launched Medvi, a GLP-1 weight-loss telehealth company, from his LA living room in September 2024 with $20K and a stack of AI tools — ChatGPT for the website, Claude for customer service, Midjourney and Runway for ad creative, ElevenLabs for voice. He outsourced all regulated medical functions to CareValidate and OpenLoop Health, keeping only the growth engine. First-year sales: $401M with a 16.2% net margin. 2026 projection: $1.8B, with two employees total (Gallagher and his brother). Sam Altman confirmed on X he’d “won a bet” with tech CEO friends about when this milestone would arrive.

The FDA issued Medvi a warning letter in February for false claims implying FDA approval of its compounded GLP-1 products — before the NYT profile ever ran. Thirty telehealth companies got the same letter. The billion-dollar milestone and the regulatory reckoning arrived in the same quarter.

Karpathy stops writing code with AI, starts building a “second brain” instead

Apr 3 Andrej Karpathy / X / DigitalToday

Karpathy revealed he’s shifted from using AI primarily for code generation to using it for knowledge management. His system: dump raw research materials into folders, point an LLM at them, and let it build and maintain an interlinked wiki from scratch — articles, backlinks, categorization, ongoing updates. His research wiki on a single topic has grown to ~100 articles and 400,000 words. No special software required — just three folders and text files. He calls it “LLM Wiki” and open-sourced the approach.

Karpathy’s argument: the highest-leverage use of LLMs isn’t generating code you’ll rewrite anyway — it’s synthesizing and organizing knowledge at a scale no human can manually maintain. The shift from “write my code” to “be my research team” is where the real productivity unlock is.

DOJ appeals ruling that blocked Trump’s federal ban on Anthropic

Apr 2 Bloomberg / NPR / US News

The Justice Department filed notice on April 2 to challenge Judge Rita Lin’s March 26 ruling that blocked the Trump administration from banning Anthropic across federal systems and labeling it a “supply chain risk.” Lin called the government’s actions “classic First Amendment retaliation” in a 43-page opinion, finding Anthropic’s due process rights were likely violated. The dispute centers on Anthropic’s refusal to remove restrictions on mass surveillance and autonomous weapons from Claude’s deployment on classified military systems — the only AI company whose model ran on the Pentagon’s classified networks.

Bloomberg’s framing: if the appeal succeeds, the precedent would let any administration punish AI companies for maintaining safety policies the government dislikes — turning acceptable-use policies into legal liabilities.

LeCun tells packed Brown auditorium: “AI sucks” — bets $1B on world models instead

Apr 1 Brown University / The Decoder / Brown Daily Herald

In a standing-room-only Lemley Family Leadership Lecture at Brown, Yann LeCun declared current AI systems “very stupid” and called investment in LLMs reaching human-level intelligence “complete BS.” He showed a slide reading “IF YOU ARE INTERESTED IN HUMAN-LEVEL AI, DON’T WORK ON LLMs.” LeCun’s alternative: world models that learn by observing physical environments, processing images, video, audio, and scientific data — not predicting the next token. His startup AMI Labs closed a $1.03B seed round (largest in European history) at a $3.5B pre-money valuation to build exactly this.

LeCun isn’t just sniping from the sidelines — he left Meta, raised a billion dollars, and is hiring. This is a Turing Award winner betting his career that the entire paradigm powering ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini is fundamentally wrong about how to reach human-level AI.
