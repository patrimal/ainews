The Definitive Take

Judge Rita Lin didn’t just grant Anthropic’s preliminary injunction — she wrote a 43-page opinion that reads like she’s already decided the final case. Calling the Pentagon’s blacklisting “classic illegal First Amendment retaliation” and the supply-chain designation “Orwellian” is about as close to telegraphing a final ruling as a federal judge gets. The government’s defense collapsed in real time during oral argument: the DOJ couldn’t explain why Anthropic was a “supply chain risk” for refusing to drop its safety redlines, couldn’t say whether Anthropic actually had the “kill switch” capability they claimed to fear, and ended up arguing that the Defense Secretary’s own “this decision is final” post wasn’t legally binding.

This is the first judicial limit on the executive branch weaponizing procurement rules against a domestic AI company over policy disagreements, and it matters far beyond Anthropic. The Pentagon tried to use a statute designed for Huawei against a company whose crime was insisting that autonomous weapons need human oversight. The court said no. Twenty-two retired generals, 149 former judges, and competitors including Microsoft and employees from OpenAI and Google DeepMind all lined up on Anthropic’s side — not because they love Anthropic, but because they understood what the precedent would mean.

The fight isn’t over. Pentagon CTO Emil Michael immediately called the ruling “a disgrace” and pointed to a separate Title 41 designation still in force, litigated in DC Circuit. The government has until roughly April 2 to seek a Ninth Circuit stay. But the legal foundation is laid: you cannot brand an American company a national security threat for publicly disagreeing with the government. That principle, once established, constrains every future administration — not just this one.

WRAP-UP

What mattered this week

Three things from this case actually change the landscape. First, Judge Lin’s First Amendment finding creates binding precedent in the Northern District of California: the government cannot retaliate against a contractor for public speech about AI safety. Every AI company that has ever worried about speaking up on policy now has case law to cite. Second, the “supply chain risk” statute (10 U.S.C. § 3252) has been judicially constrained for the first time — a tool designed for foreign adversaries cannot be repurposed to punish domestic companies for contract disputes. Third, the breadth of amicus support — Microsoft, OpenAI employees, Google DeepMind’s chief scientist, 149 former judges, 22 retired generals — demonstrated that the AI industry will close ranks when government overreach threatens the sector’s ability to self-govern on safety.

What’s unresolved: the Title 41 designation covering all federal agencies is still live and being litigated separately in DC. Emil Michael’s defiant response suggests the Pentagon will appeal. And the underlying question — whether AI companies can set safety redlines on military contracts — will ultimately be decided by the market and by Congress, not by this court. But the principle that you can’t be blacklisted for saying so publicly? That’s settled.

Breaking Defense Axios
MAR 26 — AFTERNOON

Judge Lin grants preliminary injunction: “classic illegal First Amendment retaliation”

Judge Lin issued a 43-page ruling granting Anthropic’s preliminary injunction. Key language: the Pentagon’s designation was “Orwellian” and constituted “classic illegal First Amendment retaliation” for Anthropic’s public statements about AI safety. The court found “the record strongly suggests that the reasons given for designating Anthropic a supply chain risk were pretextual and that the government’s real motive was unlawful retaliation.” Evidence cited included Trump calling Anthropic “radical left, woke” and an internal DOD memo showing the risk level escalated because Anthropic engaged through media coverage.

CNBC CNN NPR
MAR 26 — AFTERNOON

Injunction blocks 17 agencies; seven-day stay before enforcement

The injunction’s scope is broad: it blocks 17 federal agencies — including the Pentagon, State Department, and National Endowment for the Humanities — from implementing the supply chain risk designation under Title 10, Section 3252. It also bars enforcement of the president’s directive. Lin included a seven-day stay (effective ~April 2) giving the government time to seek an emergency stay from the Ninth Circuit. Notably, the ruling does not touch the separate Title 41 designation covering all federal agencies, which is being litigated in DC.

MAR 24 — 1:30 PM PT

Setting the stage: what’s at stake in San Francisco

Judge Rita Lin opens the preliminary injunction hearing in the Northern District of California. Anthropic argues the Pentagon’s “supply chain risk” designation — historically reserved for foreign adversaries like Huawei under 10 U.S.C. § 3252 — was unlawfully applied to a domestic company for refusing to drop two safety redlines: no fully autonomous lethal weapons without human oversight, and no domestic mass surveillance. Microsoft filed an amicus brief warning of “broad negative ramifications.” Twenty-two retired military officials — including former CIA/NSA director Gen. Michael Hayden, three former service secretaries, and former Navy Secretary Richard Danzig — called the designation “a misuse of government authority for retribution.” Separately, 149 former federal and state judges filed a bipartisan brief calling the DOD’s action “substantively and procedurally unlawful.”
