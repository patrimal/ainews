The Story So Far

GTC 2026 day one told a single, unmistakable story: NVIDIA is no longer a chip company — it’s an AI infrastructure monopoly that now designs every layer of the stack, from the 3nm silicon (Vera Rubin) to the inference accelerator beside it (Groq 3 LPU) to the vertical rack it sits in (Kyber) to the storage layer underneath it (BlueField-4 STX) to the software orchestrating it (NemoClaw) to — as of tonight — the actual blueprints for the buildings that house it all. The Vera Rubin DSX reference design and Omniverse DSX digital twin tool mean NVIDIA is now handing the construction industry a standardized playbook for how gigawatt-scale AI factories should be built, simulated, and operated. That’s a level of vertical integration that would make 1990s Intel blush.

The deal-making matches the ambition. Meta committed up to $27 billion over five years to Nebius Group for dedicated Vera Rubin capacity — the largest “neocloud” deal in AI history. OpenAI locked in 5 GW total. AWS committed to 1 million+ GPUs. Microsoft lit up Vera Rubin NVL72 first. Nscale is building a 1.35 GW campus in West Virginia with its own gas power plant. And every major AI lab — Anthropic, Meta, xAI, Mistral, Cohere, Perplexity, and more — has signed up as a Vera Rubin customer before a single production chip has shipped.

The supply chain is keeping pace. Samsung’s unveiling of a physical HBM4E chip — 4 TB/s bandwidth on a 4nm base die — confirmed that next-generation memory is tracking ahead of schedule while HBM4 is already in mass production for Vera Rubin. The memory bottleneck that worried the industry a year ago is being engineered away in real time. Meanwhile, NVIDIA’s push into verticals continued: healthcare got its own physical AI stack (GR00T-H for surgical robotics), BlueField-4 STX claims the storage layer for agentic AI with fifteen vendors on board, and the DSX blueprint brings Siemens, Schneider Electric, and a dozen other industrial partners into the data center design pipeline.

The market’s reaction — NVDA up just 1.65% to $183.22, drifting slightly lower after hours — tells you Wall Street had priced in most of the chip story. The real questions remain execution ones: does Vera Rubin ship on schedule in early 2027? Does that 35x tokens-per-watt Groq claim hold up in production? Can anyone actually build data centers fast enough to absorb that $1 trillion pipeline? For now, nobody else is even in the same conversation.

10:00 PM PT

Vera Rubin DSX: NVIDIA blueprints the gigawatt AI factory

NVIDIA released the Vera Rubin DSX AI Factory reference design — a standardized guide for building codesigned AI infrastructure at gigawatt scale — and made the Omniverse DSX Blueprint generally available. Engineers can now build physically accurate digital twins of their AI factories to simulate thermals, power topologies, and operations before breaking ground. DSX Flex balances energy demand with real-time grid conditions; DSX Boost claims 30% higher GPU throughput within the same power envelope. Cadence, Dassault Systèmes, Eaton, Schneider Electric, Siemens, Switch, Vertiv and others are contributing. Switch is already integrating DSX into its EVO AI Factories. This is NVIDIA designing the data center itself — not just the chips inside it.

9:00 PM PT

Samsung unveils first physical HBM4E chip — memory supply chain tracks ahead

Samsung showed a physical HBM4E chip at GTC for the first time: 16 Gbps per pin and 4.0 TB/s bandwidth, built on Samsung’s 1c DRAM process with a 4nm foundry base die. HBM4 — the generation powering Vera Rubin — is already in mass production, exceeding the 8 Gbps industry standard at 11.7 Gbps. Samsung also demonstrated hybrid copper bonding (HCB) technology enabling 16+ layer HBM stacks with 20% lower heat resistance. As the only semiconductor company offering memory, logic, foundry, and advanced packaging in-house, Samsung is positioning itself as the full-stack memory supplier for Vera Rubin and beyond.

First physical AI platform for healthcare: surgical robots get GR00T-H

NVIDIA launched the first domain-specific physical AI platform for healthcare robotics. The stack includes GR00T-H, a vision-language-action model trained on Open-H — the world’s largest open dataset for healthcare robotics, with CMR Surgical contributing nearly 500 hours of anonymized surgical video from its Versius system. Cosmos-H generates physics-based synthetic surgical data for post-training. Johnson & Johnson MedTech is already using Cosmos and Isaac for Healthcare to train its MONARCH urology platform. CMR Surgical, Moon Surgical and Rob Surgical are also adopting. This is NVIDIA’s physical AI thesis applied to one of the highest-stakes domains imaginable.

BlueField-4 STX: NVIDIA builds the storage layer for agentic AI

NVIDIA unveiled BlueField-4 STX, a modular reference architecture for accelerated storage designed specifically for agentic AI workloads. The system pairs a new storage-optimized BlueField-4 processor (combining the Vera CPU with ConnectX-9 SuperNIC) with Spectrum-X networking. NVIDIA claims 4x better energy efficiency than CPU-based storage and 5x more tokens per second via its CMX context memory platform. Fifteen major storage vendors — including Dell, HPE, IBM, NetApp, VAST Data and WEKA — are building on the architecture. STX ships second half of 2026. Another piece of the stack NVIDIA now owns: the persistent memory layer that keeps agents fast and coherent across long-running sessions.

Meta commits $27B to Nebius in largest neocloud deal ever

Meta signed a five-year, up to $27 billion AI infrastructure agreement with Nebius Group — $12 billion in dedicated capacity (including one of the first large-scale Vera Rubin deployments) plus $15 billion in additional compute. This is a ninefold expansion of the $3 billion deal they signed in November 2025, and comes days after NVIDIA invested $2 billion in Nebius. Nebius stock surged 14%. The deal underscores a new pattern: hyperscalers aren’t just building their own data centers, they’re locking up “neocloud” GPU capacity as fast as it comes online.

Kyber: NVIDIA’s next rack architecture goes vertical

Post-keynote sessions revealed details on Kyber, the successor to the Oberon rack design. Compute blades mount vertically like books on a shelf — 18 blades per chassis, up to 144 GPUs per rack, pulling 600kW with a dedicated power-and-cooling sidecar. Kyber ships with Vera Rubin Ultra in 2027, scaling from NVL72 to NVL144 to a flagship NVL576, with rack-to-rack optical interconnects enabling NVL1152 supercomputers. NVIDIA is designing the data center itself now, not just the chips inside it.

NemoClaw and the Nemotron Coalition: NVIDIA’s agentic AI play

NemoClaw is an open-source enterprise agent stack — install in one command, pair with DGX Spark or DGX Station, and deploy long-running autonomous agents locally with built-in security and privacy controls. Groq 3 LPU: the “Mellanox moment” for inference

The $20B Groq acquisition gets its coming-out party. NVIDIA will pack 256 Groq 3 LPUs into new LPX racks connected via Spectrum-X to Vera Rubin NVL72 systems — GPUs handle compute-heavy prefill, LPUs handle bandwidth-heavy token decode at 150 TB/s memory bandwidth (7x faster than Rubin alone). Vera Rubin Platform unveiled: 10x perf/watt, 88 ARM cores

The main event: NVIDIA’s next-gen Vera Rubin platform combines the Rubin GPU (3nm, HBM4) with the Vera CPU — 88 custom “Olympus” ARM cores with SMT and confidential computing, NVIDIA’s first CPU built specifically for agentic AI and reinforcement learning. Seven chips are in full production. The 1.3-million-component system delivers 10x performance per watt over Grace Blackwell. Samples ship to tier-one cloud providers late 2026, full production early 2027. Vera will also be sold standalone to compete with Intel and AMD.
