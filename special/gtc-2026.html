The Story So Far

The full picture of GTC 2026 is coming into focus, and it’s this: NVIDIA isn’t just making chips anymore — it’s designing entire data centers, from vertical rack architecture (Kyber) to the silicon inside them (Vera Rubin) to the inference accelerators bolted alongside (Groq 3 LPUs) to the software that orchestrates it all (NemoClaw). The Vera Rubin platform is the expected generational leap — 3nm, HBM4, 10x perf/watt over Grace Blackwell. But the real story is how aggressively NVIDIA is collapsing the stack: GPU, CPU, LPU, networking, rack design, power delivery, cooling. Nobody else is even attempting this.

The afternoon brought the receipts. AWS committed to deploying more than 1 million NVIDIA GPUs — Blackwell, Rubin, and Groq 3 LPUs — across its global regions. That’s not a vague partnership; that’s the world’s largest cloud provider going all-in on NVIDIA’s full stack including the new inference hardware. Roche claiming pharma’s largest GPU footprint at 3,500 Blackwell chips is a smaller number but a telling signal: AI compute is becoming as fundamental to drug discovery as wet labs.

Wall Street’s reaction was a shrug dressed as a rally — NVDA touched $188 intraday (up ~4.8%) before settling to close at $183.22, up just 1.65%. For a keynote this loaded, that tells you the market had already priced in a lot. The $1 trillion order pipeline, the Groq integration, the Kyber racks pulling 600kW — impressive on paper, but the real test is whether Vera Rubin ships on time in early 2027 and whether that 35x tokens-per-watt Groq claim survives contact with production workloads.

2:30 PM PT

Kyber: NVIDIA’s next rack architecture goes vertical

Post-keynote sessions revealed details on Kyber, the successor to the Oberon rack design. Compute blades mount vertically like books on a shelf — 18 blades per chassis, up to 144 GPUs per rack, pulling 600kW with a dedicated power-and-cooling sidecar. Kyber ships with Vera Rubin Ultra in 2027, scaling from NVL72 to NVL144 to a flagship NVL576, with rack-to-rack optical interconnects enabling NVL1152 supercomputers. NVIDIA is designing the data center itself now, not just the chips inside it.

12:15 PM PT

NemoClaw and the Nemotron Coalition: NVIDIA’s agentic AI play

NemoClaw is an open-source enterprise agent stack — install in one command, pair with DGX Spark or DGX Station, and deploy long-running autonomous agents locally with built-in security and privacy controls. Alongside it, the Nemotron Coalition brings together Mistral AI, Perplexity, Cursor, LangChain, Black Forest Labs, Reflection AI, Sarvam and Thinking Machines Lab to collaboratively build open frontier models across six families: Nemotron (language), Cosmos (world/vision), Isaac GR00T (robotics), Alpamayo (driving), BioNeMo (biology) and Earth-2 (climate).

11:45 AM PT

Groq 3 LPU: the “Mellanox moment” for inference

The $20B Groq acquisition gets its coming-out party. NVIDIA will pack 256 Groq 3 LPUs into new LPX racks connected via Spectrum-X to Vera Rubin NVL72 systems — GPUs handle compute-heavy prefill, LPUs handle bandwidth-heavy token decode at 150 TB/s memory bandwidth (7x faster than Rubin alone). Huang claims 35x tokens-per-watt improvement and explicitly compared this to the Mellanox acquisition that transformed NVIDIA’s networking story. Groq 3 chips ship Q3 2026.

11:30 AM PT

Vera Rubin Platform unveiled: 10x perf/watt, 88 ARM cores

The main event: NVIDIA’s next-gen Vera Rubin platform combines the Rubin GPU (3nm, HBM4) with the Vera CPU — 88 custom “Olympus” ARM cores with SMT and confidential computing, NVIDIA’s first CPU built specifically for agentic AI and reinforcement learning. Seven chips are in full production. The 1.3-million-component system delivers 10x performance per watt over Grace Blackwell. Samples ship to tier-one cloud providers late 2026, full production early 2027. Vera will also be sold standalone to compete with Intel and AMD.
